What Happens Now

The closing is done — but several things wrap up behind the scenes over the next few weeks. Here's exactly what to expect and when.

  • 1–3
    days

    Deed Recording

    The title company submits the deed to Greene County for recording. Once recorded, the transfer is official public record. You can verify at the Greene County Auditor's website.

  • 1–2
    wks

    Mortgage Payoff Confirmation

    If you had a mortgage, your lender will process the payoff from your sale proceeds. You'll receive a final statement confirming your loan is paid in full — keep this document forever.

  • 2–4
    wks

    Escrow Refund

    If you had an escrow account, your lender will refund the remaining balance — usually within 20–30 business days of payoff. Watch for a check at your new address.

  • 30
    days

    Property Tax Records Update

    Greene County will update their tax records to reflect the new owner. Your responsibility for property taxes ends on your closing date — the settlement statement shows the exact proration.

  • 60
    days

    Full Closing Document Package

    You should receive your complete closing document package from the title company. Store these safely — both physical and digital copies.

  • Jan
    next yr

    1099-S Tax Document

    You may receive a 1099-S reporting the sale. Share this with your CPA. Most sellers qualify for the primary residence capital gains exclusion — ask your tax professional about your specific situation.

Please note: Timelines above are general estimates. Always confirm specific dates and amounts with your title company, lender, or closing attorney. For tax questions, please consult a qualified CPA or tax advisor.

Financial Next Steps

You just received significant proceeds from your sale. Here's a smart framework for thinking about what comes next.

💰 What to Do With Your Proceeds

  • Park it safely first. Move proceeds to a high-yield savings account (HYSA) or money market account — don't let it sit in a standard checking account while you decide next steps.
  • Pay off high-interest debt. Credit cards, personal loans, or car loans — this is often the highest guaranteed "return" available.
  • Fund your next home purchase. If you're buying again, keep what you need for down payment + closing costs + reserves liquid and accessible.
  • Talk to a financial advisor. A one-time session with a fee-only advisor is worth every penny to make sure these proceeds are working hard for you.

🧾 Tax Considerations

  • Primary Residence Exclusion: If you lived in the home as your primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years, you may exclude up to $500,000 of gain (married filing jointly) from capital gains tax.
  • Deductible Selling Costs: Commission, title fees, attorney fees, and some repairs done specifically for the sale may reduce your taxable gain — keep all receipts.
  • Cost Basis: Your original purchase price plus improvements you made over the years equals your cost basis. The higher your basis, the lower your taxable gain.
  • 1031 Exchange: If this was an investment property, ask your CPA about a 1031 exchange to defer capital gains by reinvesting in another property.

Seller FAQ

The questions I hear most from sellers after closing. And if yours isn't here — just text me!

When will I get my escrow refund? ▼
Your lender typically processes the escrow refund within 20–30 business days after receiving your payoff. It usually comes as a check mailed to your address on file — update your address with your lender ASAP! For specific timing on your account, contact your lender directly.
Do I owe capital gains tax on the sale? ▼
Potentially, but most sellers qualify for the IRS primary residence exclusion — up to $500,000 of gain for married couples filing jointly if you lived in the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years. Please consult your CPA or tax advisor to confirm your specific situation — this is general information only, not tax advice.
When should I cancel my homeowner's insurance? ▼
Only after confirming the deed has recorded and transferred out of your name — typically 1–3 business days after closing. Call your insurance agent for a pro-rated refund on unused premium. Confirm deed transfer before canceling — contact your title company if unsure.
What if the buyer finds an issue after closing? ▼
In Ohio, sellers are generally protected once the sale is complete as long as all required disclosures were made accurately. If you receive any communication from the buyer post-closing, contact me immediately before responding to anything — and consider consulting a real estate attorney. This is general information, not legal advice. Please consult a licensed Ohio real estate attorney for your specific situation.
What if mail keeps going to my old address? ▼
Make sure USPS mail forwarding is active at usps.com/move — it takes 7–10 business days to kick in. Then systematically update every account in the Address Updates tab. The new owners may receive stray mail for a few months — that's normal.
